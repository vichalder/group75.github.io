The Larceny Wave

How reported larceny theft took shape across San Francisco from 2003 to 2025

Data Story Larceny Theft 2003–2025 3 Visualizations SFPD Dataset

For more than two decades, reported larceny theft has left a pattern across San Francisco that is difficult to miss once it comes into view. It gathers in some parts of the city far more than others. It rises during the hours when streets, shops, sidewalks, and parked cars are busiest. It also changes sharply over time, swelling in the mid to late 2010s, falling suddenly in 2020, and recovering only part of that lost ground afterward. Those patterns appear in incident reports from the San Francisco Police Department, published through DataSF, covering the years from 2003 to 2025. DataSF describes this dataset as useful for counting incident reports by type, date, time, and location, which makes it a strong source for showing where and when reported larceny clusters. In this dataset, larceny includes non-violent theft of property and many thefts from vehicles, which makes it one of the clearest categories for tracking how theft overlaps with everyday urban movement. Still, these records do not capture every theft that happened. They show only incidents that were reported. That means changes in the numbers do not always reflect changes in theft itself. Some of the movement in the data may come from the system that produced the records. That matters even more here because the long time series spans both the historical incident dataset and the newer post-2018 system. Even with those limits in view, the broad story remains clear. Reported larceny in San Francisco is not scattered at random. It follows a pattern across time and place.

Part 1

Twenty Years of Theft

For much of the 2000s, reported larceny theft in San Francisco changed relatively little from year to year. From 2003 to 2011, the annual total stayed within a fairly narrow band, usually around the mid twenty-thousands.

Beginning in the early 2010s, the pattern shifts. Reported incidents rise steadily through the middle of the decade and reach a peak of 62,577 in 2018. That rise overlaps with a period when San Francisco was also drawing many more visitors and seeing a much stronger labor market. More than 16 million people visited the city in 2011 (Finz, 2012), while 2018 brought 25.8 million visitors (Travel Courier, 2019). Over roughly the same period, the unemployment rate in San Francisco County and City fell from about 9 percent in early 2011 to about 4 percent in early 2018 (Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, 2026). Taken together, those shifts point to a city that was becoming busier, more crowded, and more active in everyday life.

That context matters because the rise in larceny also overlaps with San Francisco’s well-documented vehicle break-in boom. Public attention to the problem had become intense by 2017, when the city recorded more than 31,000 reported car break-ins (Palomino et al., 2018). The chart does not prove that tourism or economic recovery caused the increase, but both help explain why opportunities for theft may have grown. A busier city brings more movement, more visitors, and more cars parked in public space.

The series changes direction abruptly in 2020. Reported larceny drops sharply in the first pandemic year, which fits the sudden slowdown in everyday activity across the city. In the years that follow, totals rise again, but they do not return to the late 2010s high point. This is why the figure works best as a record of changing patterns in reported incidents, not as a complete account of theft itself. The overall shape is clear even if the reasons behind it remain more complex.

Part 2

Where Theft Concentrates

Zoomed out on the full city map, it is difficult to clearly identify changes in larceny crime patterns. At this scale, the dominant impression is simply the overall increase in incidents, as already shown in the previous graph. However, the map allows for a closer examination of how these crimes are distributed geographically, and how that distribution has shifted over time.

Citywide, the overall level of crime in 2012 and 2020 appears broadly similar. When looking at individual districts, however, substantial differences become visible. In 2012, larceny incidents are highly concentrated in central areas, particularly around the Tenderloin, where levels are notably high. By 2020, this pattern changes significantly, with the Tenderloin showing much lower levels of reported incidents.

A general pattern emerging during the COVID-19 period and the years following is that larceny theft becomes more spatially dispersed. Rather than clustering in dense, high-activity areas, incidents are spread more evenly across the city. This shift likely reflects changes in routine activity patterns during the pandemic, with fewer people commuting to central districts and more time spent in residential neighborhoods. As a result, opportunities for vehicle-related theft became less concentrated in traditional hotspots and more distributed across a wider urban area.

Part 3

The Thief's Clock: When Theft Happens

The heat map makes it possible to see how larceny theft varies across hours of the day and days of the week. Rather than being evenly distributed, incidents follow a consistent daily rhythm that repeats throughout the week.

Across all days, larceny theft is relatively low during the early morning hours. Activity begins to increase through the late morning and reaches its highest levels in the afternoon and early evening. This pattern is especially pronounced between roughly midday and early evening, where the concentration of incidents is consistently high.

There are also noticeable differences between weekdays and weekends. While the general daily rhythm remains similar, Fridays and Saturdays stand out with higher overall levels of reported incidents, particularly during the afternoon and evening hours. This suggests that periods of increased social and commercial activity are associated with more opportunities for theft.

What makes the heat map particularly compelling is how strongly it challenges the common assumption that theft mainly occurs at night. In this dataset, reported larceny follows the active city more than the sleeping city. Incidents are most frequent when people are out shopping, commuting, meeting, parking, unloading, and moving between places. The busiest periods appear to depend less on darkness and more on the presence of people and property in public space.

This interpretation also connects to the earlier spatial patterns observed during the COVID-19 period. At that time, overall larceny levels fell and incidents became less concentrated in central areas, as commuting declined, tourism dropped, and fewer cars and belongings were left in public space. In other words, when the city’s daily activity patterns changed, so did the opportunities for theft. The same underlying logic is visible here: larceny theft is closely tied to when and where people and property are present, rather than to time of day in itself.

Figure 3. Heatmap of reported larceny theft by day of week and hour of day from 2003 to 2025. Darker cells indicate more recorded incidents. The strongest pattern is a broad afternoon concentration across the week, with especially high counts on Friday and Saturday. This pattern is consistent with routine activity theory, which links crime to the meeting of suitable targets and offenders in everyday settings. The figure suggests that reported larceny follows the timing of everyday urban activity more than the common image of crime as something that mainly happens after dark.

Conclusion

Taken together, the three figures tell a clear story. Over the last two decades, reported larceny theft in San Francisco rose sharply in the mid to late 2010s, clustered in districts shaped by dense commercial and visitor activity, and appeared most often during the hours when the city was busiest. Read side by side, the charts suggest a form of theft shaped by the flow of daily city life.

The story becomes stronger, not weaker, when its limits are stated plainly. Incident reports are not the same as all thefts that occurred. A district map based on concentration is not the same as a population adjusted map of neighborhood harm. A heatmap of recorded incidents cannot reveal motive on its own. Even so, the value of the dataset is real. It makes structure visible. It shows that reported larceny in San Francisco is not random noise in the background of city life. Still, these records do not capture every theft that happened. They show only incidents that entered the official record. For that reason, a rise or fall in the data does not map perfectly onto a rise or fall in theft itself.
